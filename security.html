Boundary

The most important question is not “do you have security features,” but “what data enters the system and under what rules.”

PHI boundary and intended use

Homeroom’s intended workflow is for internal knowledge content such as approved policies, clinical protocols, onboarding materials, and operating procedures. That workflow is designed so organizations do not need PHI to receive value from the product.

Current stance

PHI should not be pasted into the production knowledge workflow. On this site, the demo treats PHI as unsafe input and redacts it locally in the browser before any publish or model-adjacent step is allowed.

What this means for vendor risk

  • The operational knowledge use case is separated from patient-specific content.
  • Clinical staff get easier-to-read guidance without creating a new repository of patient records.
  • The review focus shifts to governance, auditability, and access control around approved knowledge assets.

Encryption

The site should answer how content is protected in transit and at rest, even when the target workload is non-PHI operational knowledge.

Encryption posture

  • Transport encryption for browser-to-service traffic via TLS.
  • Encryption at rest for persisted application content and metadata.
  • Role-based access boundaries applied before content presentation.
  • Audit visibility on page ownership, approvals, and changes to controlled content.

HIPAA / BAA

This is where the buyer wants precision. Table-stakes claims are not enough without the operating rule behind them.

HIPAA and BAA stance

Homeroom’s core knowledge workflow is intended to operate without PHI. Because that workflow is designed around non-PHI operational content, a BAA is not required for the default use case described on this site.

If the workflow changes

If a customer’s intended use evolves so that PHI would be processed or stored as part of the product workflow, the PHI handling boundary must be redefined before production use, including execution of a BAA and updated technical controls appropriate to that scope.

Why the demo still matters

Even when the intended workflow excludes PHI, real-world teams test the edges. The demo exists so a reviewer can intentionally paste patient identifiers and see them masked before the content is allowed to move any further.

Flow

For risk reviewers, an understandable data path is often more persuasive than a broad claim.

End-to-end content flow on this site

Step 1 User pastes draft clinical or training content into the demo input.
Step 2 Local detector scans for names, DOB, MRN, SSN, phone, email, and address patterns.
Step 3 Identifiers are replaced with explicit redaction tokens in the browser.
Step 4 Only sanitized text is allowed into the governed wiki preview.
Step 5 Reviewer sees owner, audience, approval chain, and audit entries around the final page.

Retention and segregation model

  • Operational knowledge content is managed separately from patient record systems.
  • Access is intended to be role-based and limited to organizational audiences defined by the customer.
  • Customer review should include retention policy, environment separation, and access evidence as part of vendor diligence.

Evidence

Security reviews go faster when the site tells reviewers what evidence exists and what to ask for next.

Evidence package to provide in diligence

  • SOC 2 materials and control mapping available for review.
  • Written HIPAA posture and documented PHI boundary.
  • Architecture diagram covering ingress, redaction, storage, and access paths.
  • Access control model, audit logging examples, and ownership workflow.
  • Retention, deletion, and environment segregation documentation.

Reviewer shortcut

Open the live PHI-redaction demo, paste a realistic sample, and confirm the raw identifiers are masked before the governed wiki preview is allowed to render.
